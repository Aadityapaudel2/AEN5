title: "Gold Eclipse Problem v21.23"

  description: "At 21:20, the Gold Eclipse Crown vanished from its secured vault in the archives. Four individuals (1, 2, 3, 4) each had valid key‑badge records near that time. Exactly one suspect lies in all of their statements, while the other three speak only truths. Only one suspect is actually guilty. Each suspect makes four statements:

    Suspect 1:
      (1) 'I left the vault area at 21:10, so I'm not guilty.'
      (2) '2 was still near the Crown at 21:15.'
      (3) 'If 3 is lying, then 4 must definitely be the culprit.'
      (4) 'I saw 3 in the corridor at 21:05.'

    Suspect 2:
      (1) '1 did not leave at 21:10; in fact, 1 was there until 21:25.'
      (2) 'I never touched the Gold Eclipse Crown after 21:00.'
      (3) '4 was definitely at the Crown at 21:15, which clashes with 1’s claim.'
      (4) 'If 3 is telling truth, then 1’s statements must be false.'

    Suspect 3:
      (1) 'I was nowhere near the vault after 21:05.'
      (2) 'Either 2 is guilty, or 1 is lying about leaving early — but not both.'
      (3) '4 is not a thief; I saw them in another wing at 21:15.'
      (4) 'If 1 is truthful, then 2’s timeline is incorrect.'

    Suspect 4:
      (1) '3 was definitely near the vault at 21:05, contradicting 3’s denial.'
      (2) 'If 1 is lying, then 2 is innocent.'
      (3) 'I am not guilty; 2’s logs confirm I was elsewhere.'
      (4) 'If 2 is lying, 3 must be guilty or 1 must have lied, not sure which.'

  question: "Which single suspect (1,2,3,4) stole the Gold Eclipse Crown? Provide one digit only."

answer: >
  explanation: "Formal Predicate Logic:

  Let L(x) = 'x lies in all statements,' T(x) = 'x is fully truthful in all statements,' and G(x) = 'x is guilty.'
  Exactly one suspect L(x)=true, exactly one suspect G(x)=true, the other three T(x).

  Case L(1):
    Then 1’s (1),(2),(3),(4) are all false => 1 did not leave at 21:10 => possible guilt, 2 was not near Crown 21:15, etc. Meanwhile 2,3,4 are truthful => timeline conflicts quickly arise, e.g. 2(1) says 1 left 21:25 vs 4(2) referencing 1’s honesty. Contradiction.

  Case L(2):
    Then 2’s (1),(2),(3),(4) are false =>
    - (1) false => 1 indeed left 21:10, not 21:25
    - (2) false => 2 must have touched the Crown after 21:00 => suspicious
    - (3) false => 4 was not definitely at Crown 21:15 => no clash with 1(2)
    - (4) false => if 3 is truthful => 1’s statements are not necessarily false
    Then 1,3,4 are truthful => 1(1) 'Left 21:10 => not guilty' => G(1)=false, 3(2) 'Either 2 is guilty or 1 is lying about leaving but not both' => 1 is not lying => 2 is guilty => consistent. No contradiction emerges.

  Case L(3):
    Then 3’s (1),(2),(3),(4) all false => 3 was near vault after 21:05, so maybe guilt, etc. 1,2,4 are truthful => 2(3) says '4 at Crown 21:15' might or might not conflict with 1(2). Typically leads to mismatch. Contradiction.

  Case L(4):
    Then 4’s (1),(2),(3),(4) are false => 1,2,3 truthful => times or blame produce contradictions with 3(2) or 1(4). Fails.

  Only L(2) scenario stands => G(2) must be the culprit. So suspect 2 stole the Gold Eclipse Crown."

  final: "2"
